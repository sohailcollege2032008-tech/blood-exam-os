Q23 Written

Compare between innate and acquired immunity

Innate Immunity Characteristics — 8:
1)Components — physical/chemical barriers, phagocytes, NK cells, complement
proteins.
2)Activity type — continuously active since birth.
3)Response speed — immediate onset.
4)Potency — limited effectiveness.
5)Time span — lifelong duration.
6)Inheritance — genetically inherited.
7)Specificity — non-specific target recognition.
8)Memory — absent immunologic memory.
Adaptive (Acquired) Immunity Characteristics — 8:
1)Components — B cells (humoral) + T cells (cell-mediated).
2)Activity type — silent mode until antigen identification.
3)Response speed — delayed onset.
4)Potency — extremely high effectiveness.
5)Time span — short or lifelong duration.
6)Inheritance — not genetically inherited.
7)Specificity — highly specific target recognition.
8)Memory — present immunologic memory.
Q24 Written

Mention the mechanism and clinical examples of Type- III hypersensitivity?

Pathophysiological Mechanism — 3:
1)Soluble complex formation — IgG or IgM bind antigens — escape phagocytosis.
2)Vascular deposition — complexes penetrate endothelium — lodge in basement
membrane.
3)Inflammatory tissue destruction — complement activation + PMN infiltration
(C3a/C5a) + platelet microthrombi.
Systemic Clinical Examples — 3:
1)Serum sickness — follows foreign serum/drug injection.
2)Autoimmune diseases — rheumatoid arthritis + systemic lupus erythematosus
(SLE).
3)Post-infection conditions — glomerulonephritis + pneumonitis.
Local Clinical Examples — 1:
1)Arthus reaction — follows repeated subcutaneous antigen injection.
Q25 Written

Mention mechanisms of tumor escape from immune recognition?

Active evasion mechanisms — 4:
1)Immunosuppressive factor production — tumor secretes inhibitory mediators.
2)Suppressor T-cell stimulation — tumor factors activate regulatory cells.
3)Antigen shedding — released antigens block T-cell receptors.
4)Immunological enhancement (masking) — specific antibodies cover tumor
antigens — blocks cytotoxic cells.
Passive evasion mechanisms — 2:
1)Antigen absence — tumor cells lack surface antigen expression.
2)Immunologically privileged sites — tumor grows in inaccessible anatomical
areas (e.g., CNS).

Q43 Written

Enumerate why intravascular clotting does not occur under physiological conditions?

Endothelial factors — 4:
1)Smooth endothelial surface — prevents platelet adhesion + activation
2)Glycocalyx layer — repels platelets + clotting factors
3)Thrombomodulin — binds thrombin → activates Protein C
4)Prostacyclin (PGI2) + Nitric oxide (NO) — inhibit platelet aggregation +
induce vasodilation
Plasma anticoagulants — 3:
1)Antithrombin III — binds endogenous heparin → inactivates thrombin + factors
IXa, Xa, XIa, XIIa
2)Protein C + Protein S complex — inactivates factors Va + VIIIa
3)Tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) — converts plasminogen to plasmin →
initiates continuous fibrinolysis
Hemodynamic factors — 1:
1)Continuous rapid blood flow — dilutes + clears locally activated clotting
factors
Q44 Written

Describe incompatible blood transfusion reactions (Major incompatible reactions)?

Immunological mechanisms — 3:
1)Type II hypersensitivity — preformed recipient antibodies (agglutinins)
attack donor RBC antigens
2)Agglutination — clumped RBCs occlude small capillaries
3)Complement activation — triggers rapid intravascular hemolysis → massive
release of free hemoglobin
Systemic manifestations — 4:
1)Hemolytic jaundice — excessive RBC destruction exceeds liver bilirubin
clearance
2)Fever + chills — triggered by pyrogen release from destroyed cells +
macrophages
3)Chest/back pain + dyspnea — due to microvascular occlusion in lungs +
tissues
4)Circulatory shock — massive release of inflammatory mediators (bradykinin,
histamine) → profound hypotension
Renal complications (Acute Renal Failure) — 3:
1)Renal ischemia — profound sympathetic vasoconstriction secondary to shock
2)Tubular obstruction — free hemoglobin precipitates in acidic renal tubules
3)Acute tubular necrosis — direct toxic effect of excess free hemoglobin on
epithelium

Q57 Written

What is sickle hemoglobin [1].

Structural mutation — 2:
1)Abnormal hemoglobin variant (HbS) — causes sickle cell disease
2)Missense point mutation — glutamate replaced by valine — at position 6 of
beta-globin chain
Pathophysiological mechanisms — 3:
1)Deoxygenated HbS — polymerizes into insoluble rigid fibers — distorts RBCs
into sickle shape
2)Microvascular occlusion — sickled RBCs block capillaries — causes tissue
ischemia and pain crises
3)Premature RBC destruction — fragile sickled cells rapidly sequestered —
leads to hemolytic anemia
Genetic states — 2:
1)Homozygous state (HbSS) — full sickle cell disease — severe clinical
phenotype
2)Heterozygous state (HbAS) — sickle cell trait — confers resistance to
*Plasmodium falciparum* malaria
